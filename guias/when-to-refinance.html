When Does Refinancing Actually Make Sense?

Forget the old “1% rule.” Whether a refinance saves or costs you money comes down to one number — your break-even point — and how long you’ll stay. Here’s the real math.

Refinancing replaces your current mortgage with a new one, usually to get a lower rate. But it isn’t free, and a lower rate doesn’t automatically mean you come out ahead. The deciding factor is almost always how long it takes your monthly savings to repay the cost of refinancing.

The break-even point: the only number that matters

The break-even point is how many months it takes for your monthly savings to repay the closing costs of the refinance. The formula is simple:

The formula

Break-even (months) = Closing costs ÷ Monthly savings. If you spend $6,000 to refinance and save $200 a month, you break even in 30 months. Stay past that and the savings are yours; sell or move before it and you’ve lost money.

Here’s a real example. Say you owe $390,000 at 7.75% (a 2023-era rate), paying about $2,792 a month in principal and interest. Refinancing to 6.5% drops that to roughly $2,465 — a savings of $327 a month. With closing costs around $9,000, your break-even is $9,000 ÷ $327 = about 27 months. If you’ll stay in the home five or more years, the math clearly works.

Why the “1% rule” is outdated

You’ve probably heard that refinancing is only worth it if you can drop your rate by a full percentage point. That rule is a leftover from an era of smaller loan balances, and it can talk you out of a perfectly good refinance.

The reason it fails: savings scale with loan size. A 0.5% drop on a $200,000 loan might take 11 years to break even — not worth it. The same 0.5% drop on a $500,000 loan could break even in two years. With today’s median home prices above $400,000, even a 0.5–0.75% reduction can generate $150–$200 a month in savings.

A better rule of thumb

If your rate drop is under 0.5%, be skeptical. If it’s 1% or more, it’s almost certainly worth running the numbers. Anywhere in between depends on your balance, your closing costs, and how long you’ll stay — which is exactly what the break-even calculation captures and a blanket rule can’t.

What refinancing actually costs

Refinance closing costs typically run 2% to 5% of the loan amount. On a $300,000 refinance, that’s roughly $6,000 to $15,000. The main line items:

CostTypical range
Origination fee0.5–1% of loan
Appraisal$400–$700
Title insurance & search$700–$2,000
Escrow & recordingVaries by state

You’re legally entitled to a Loan Estimate from each lender within three business days of applying — it itemizes every fee, and it costs nothing to request several and compare. A “no-closing-cost” refinance is also an option: the lender covers the fees in exchange for a slightly higher rate, which can make sense if you expect to refinance or sell again within a few years.

The term-reset trap

Here’s the mistake that quietly costs people money: refinancing a loan with 24 years left into a fresh 30-year term. Your monthly payment drops — partly from the lower rate, partly because you’ve stretched repayment back out over 30 years. But that longer term can mean you pay more total interest over the life of the loan, even at a lower rate.

Always compare lifetime interest, not just the monthly payment. If you refinance, consider matching your remaining term (or going shorter) rather than automatically resetting to 30 years. A shorter term raises the monthly payment but can save tens of thousands in interest and build equity faster.

Good reasons to refinance beyond rate

Lowering your rate is the classic reason, but not the only one:

Dropping PMI. If your home has appreciated and you now have 20% equity, refinancing into a conventional loan removes mortgage insurance — especially valuable for escaping FHA’s permanent MIP. Switching from adjustable to fixed. If you have an ARM and want predictability, locking a fixed rate can be worth it even without a big rate drop. Shortening the term. Moving from a 30-year to a 15-year loan sharply cuts total interest. Cash-out refinancing. Tapping equity for renovations or debt consolidation — though it increases your loan, and lenders typically cap cash-out at 80% of your home’s value.
