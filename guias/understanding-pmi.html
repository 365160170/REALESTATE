Understanding PMI: What It Costs and How to Get Rid of It

Private mortgage insurance protects your lender, not you — but you’re the one paying for it. Here’s exactly what drives the cost, how much you can expect to pay, and every way to remove it.

If you buy with less than 20% down on a conventional loan, you’ll almost certainly pay private mortgage insurance (PMI). It’s an extra monthly charge that builds none of your equity — which is exactly why it’s worth understanding how to minimize and eliminate it.

What PMI is (and why it exists)

When you put down less than 20%, the lender is taking on more risk — you have less of your own money in the home, which statistically makes default more likely. PMI is an insurance policy that covers the lender if you stop paying. You pay the premiums, but the payout goes to them. It’s the price of admission for a low-down-payment loan.

The upside: PMI is what makes buying with 3–5% down possible at all. Without it, low-down-payment conventional loans wouldn’t exist. The goal isn’t to fear PMI — it’s to pay as little as possible and shed it as soon as you can.

How much PMI costs — by credit score

PMI typically runs between 0.3% and 1.5% of your loan amount per year, divided into 12 monthly payments. Unlike FHA insurance, PMI is credit-sensitive — your credit score has a dramatic effect on the rate. Here’s roughly what a borrower with 5% down on a $380,000 loan might pay:

780+~0.35%~$111~$1,330
720~0.75%~$238~$2,850
680~1.15%~$364~$4,370
Why this matters

The gap between a 680 and a 780 score on the same loan can be over $250 a month — roughly $3,000 a year — for the exact same house. Before you save another dollar for a bigger down payment, it’s often worth checking whether a 40–60 point credit-score improvement would cut your PMI in half.

Your down payment matters too: the less you put down, the higher the PMI rate. Every 5% you add reduces the cost, though the savings shrink as you approach 20%.

The four ways to remove PMI

PMI isn’t permanent on a conventional loan. There are four paths to getting rid of it:

1. Automatic termination at 78% LTV

By federal law (the Homeowners Protection Act), your servicer must automatically cancel PMI once your loan balance reaches 78% of the home’s original value, as long as you’re current on payments. This happens on its own, based on your original amortization schedule — no action required.

2. Request cancellation at 80% LTV

You don’t have to wait for 78%. Once you reach 20% equity (80% LTV), you can submit a written request to cancel PMI early. Servicers typically require a solid payment history and no second liens. On a $300,000 home, that’s requesting cancellation when your balance hits $240,000 — potentially months earlier than automatic termination.

3. Get a new appraisal after your home appreciates

If your home’s value has risen, you may hit 20% equity faster than your payments alone would suggest. A new appraisal (which you pay for, usually $400–$700) can prove it and trigger cancellation ahead of schedule.

4. Refinance below 80% LTV

If you have 20%+ equity based on current value, refinancing into a new conventional loan wipes out PMI. This only makes sense if the new rate and closing costs work in your favor — check the math with our refinance calculator first.

PMI vs FHA’s MIP — an important difference

This trips up a lot of buyers. FHA loans don’t charge PMI — they charge MIP (mortgage insurance premium), and the rules are very different. On most FHA loans with less than 10% down, MIP lasts for the entire life of the loan and never cancels. FHA also charges a 1.75% upfront premium (about $6,755 on a $386,000 loan), usually rolled into the balance.

The takeaway

Conventional PMI is temporary and cancellable. FHA MIP usually isn’t. This is the single biggest reason buyers with decent credit often choose conventional loans even when FHA allows a lower down payment — the ability to shed insurance is worth real money over time.

Should you avoid PMI entirely?

The surest way to avoid PMI is a 20% down payment. But waiting years to save that much has its own cost — rising home prices and rent paid in the meantime can easily exceed what you’d spend on a few years of PMI. For many buyers, paying PMI for a while and buying now beats waiting.

The smart move is to treat PMI as a temporary cost with a clear exit plan: know your cancellation threshold, track your equity, and remove it the moment you qualify.
