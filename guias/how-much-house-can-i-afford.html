How Much House Can You Really Afford?

The number a lender approves and the number you can comfortably live with are rarely the same. Here’s how to find both — and why you should buy to the smaller one.

“How much house can I afford?” has two answers. There’s the lender’s answer — the maximum they’ll approve based on ratios — and your answer, which factors in the life you actually want to live. Understanding the gap between them is the difference between owning a home and being owned by one.

How lenders decide: the 28/36 rule

Lenders judge affordability mainly through your debt-to-income ratio (DTI) — the percentage of your gross monthly income that goes to debt. The classic benchmark is the 28/36 rule:

28%
Max of gross income on housing (front-end ratio)
36%
Max on total debt including housing (back-end)
43%
Common ceiling many lenders won’t exceed

So if you earn $9,000 a month gross, the 28% guideline puts housing around $2,520 and the 36% guideline caps total debt around $3,240. Some programs stretch the back-end number — FHA sometimes allows up to 50% — but higher ratios mean a thinner monthly cushion. Your credit score, down payment, and loan type also shape the final number, but DTI usually sets the ceiling.

What counts as debt (and what doesn’t)

DTI only includes debts that show up on your credit report. That’s a narrower list than your real budget:

Counts as debtDoes NOT count
Car loansGroceries
Student loansUtilities
Minimum credit-card paymentsSubscriptions
Personal loans, child supportInsurance, phone bills
The catch

This is exactly why the lender’s maximum can feel too high. DTI ignores everyday living costs that are very real — daycare, groceries, that gym membership — so two people with identical DTI can have wildly different amounts of actual breathing room.

Qualifying vs comfortable

The maximum a lender approves is a ceiling, not a target. It ignores retirement savings, travel, hobbies, and the emergencies that make real life more expensive than a loan application. A payment that technically “qualifies” can still leave you house-poor — unable to save, stressed by every unexpected bill.

A healthier approach: find your lender maximum, then ask what monthly payment lets you keep saving and still enjoy your life. Buy to the smaller number. Plenty of financially secure homeowners deliberately borrow well below what they could.

Why the full payment changes everything

Affordability is about PITI — Principal, Interest, Taxes, and Insurance — plus PMI and any HOA dues, not just the mortgage. This matters more than buyers expect, because taxes and insurance vary enormously by location.

Two homes at the same $360,000 price can have monthly payments hundreds of dollars apart if one sits in a high-property-tax state and the other doesn’t. A home that looks affordable on the sticker price can blow your budget once the full PITI is counted — always estimate the complete payment before you fall in love.

The levers that change what you can afford

If the number comes up short, a few things move it:

Paying down debt. Clearing a car loan or credit-card balance directly lowers your DTI and raises your ceiling — sometimes more than a raise would. A bigger down payment. More cash down means a smaller loan and a lower payment, plus the chance to avoid PMI at 20%. Improving your credit score. A better score lowers your rate and your PMI, which lowers your payment and lifts what you qualify for. A longer term. Stretching to a 30-year loan lowers the monthly payment (though it raises total interest).
