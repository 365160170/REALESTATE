The First-Time Home Buyer’s Guide

Everything you need to go from renting to holding the keys — loan types, down payments, hidden costs, and the exact order to do things in. No jargon, no fluff.

Buying your first home feels overwhelming because it is genuinely a lot of moving parts at once: your credit, your savings, loan programs, an emotional search, and a stack of paperwork. But it breaks down into a clear sequence. Do these steps in order and the process stops feeling like guesswork.

1. Figure out your real budget first

The first question isn’t “which house?” — it’s “how much house?” Shopping before you know your number is how people fall in love with homes they can’t comfortably afford. Lenders answer this question with your debt-to-income ratio (DTI), the share of your gross monthly income that goes to debt payments.

The common benchmark is the 28/36 rule: keep housing costs at or below 28% of gross monthly income, and total debt (housing plus car loans, student loans, credit cards) at or below 36%. Many loan programs stretch the back-end number higher — FHA loans sometimes allow up to 43–50% — but the closer you stay to 36%, the more breathing room your monthly budget has.

Do this first

Run your income and existing debts through our affordability calculator to turn your salary into a realistic price ceiling. Then ask a second question the calculator can’t: what monthly payment lets you still save and live? Buy to the smaller of the two numbers.

There’s a difference between what a lender will approve and what you can comfortably carry. Approval ignores retirement savings, travel, childcare, and the emergencies that make real life more expensive than a loan application. Plenty of financially healthy buyers deliberately borrow well below their maximum.

2. Understand your loan options

Most first-time buyers end up choosing between four loan types. The right one depends mostly on your credit score, how much cash you have, and whether you qualify for special programs. Here’s how they compare:

Loan typeMin. downMin. creditMortgage insurance
Conventional3–5%620PMI, cancels at 20% equity
FHA3.5%580MIP, often for the life of the loan
VA0%No official min.None
USDA0%640 (typical)Guarantee fee

Conventional loans are the default for buyers with decent credit. First-time-buyer programs like HomeReady and Home Possible allow as little as 3% down. The big advantage: once you reach 20% equity, PMI comes off and your payment drops — something FHA can’t usually offer.

FHA loans are built for buyers with lower credit or a thin credit history. You can qualify with a score as low as 580 (3.5% down), or 500–579 with 10% down. The catch is mortgage insurance: FHA charges a 1.75% upfront premium plus an annual premium (around 0.55%) that, on most FHA loans, sticks for the life of the loan unless you refinance out.

VA loans are, for those who qualify, usually the best deal in American lending: zero down, no mortgage insurance, and typically lower rates. They’re exclusive to veterans, active-duty service members, and some surviving spouses. If you’re eligible, start here. USDA loans also offer zero down for homes in eligible rural and suburban areas, with income limits.

A common strategy

Many buyers use an FHA loan to get in the door with a low down payment and looser credit rules, then refinance into a conventional loan once they’ve built 20% equity and improved their credit — shedding the mortgage insurance and often landing a lower rate.

3. How much you actually need for a down payment

The “20% down” figure is the most persistent myth in home buying. It’s a great target — it removes PMI and strengthens your offer — but it is not a requirement. On a $360,000 home, here’s what different down payments actually look like:

Down paymentCash neededLoan amountPMI?
3%$10,800$349,200Yes
5%$18,000$342,000Yes
10%$36,000$324,000Yes (lower)
20%$72,000$288,000No

The right down payment usually isn’t the biggest one you can scrape together — it’s the one that leaves you with a comfortable payment and an intact emergency fund. Draining every dollar of savings to hit 20% and then facing a broken water heater in month two is a worse position than paying PMI for a few years with cash in the bank.

Don’t overlook down payment assistance programs. Nearly every state and many cities offer grants or low-interest second loans for first-time buyers, often stackable with FHA loans. And note the technical definition: for most programs, “first-time buyer” simply means you haven’t owned a home in the last three years — so previous owners can qualify again.

4. The costs nobody warns you about

The down payment gets all the attention, but it’s only part of the cash you need. Budget for these too:

Closing costs (2–5% of the loan)

These are the fees to finalize the loan — appraisal, title insurance, origination, recording, and prepaid escrow for taxes and insurance. On a $342,000 loan, that’s roughly $6,800 to $17,000 due at closing, on top of your down payment. Some can be negotiated or covered by the seller.

The full monthly payment (PITI)

Your payment isn’t just principal and interest. It’s PITI: Principal, Interest, Taxes, and Insurance — plus PMI if you’re under 20% down, plus HOA dues if applicable. Two identical home prices in different towns can carry very different tax bills, and that changes what you can afford. Always estimate the full payment, not just the mortgage. Our mortgage calculator bundles all of these in.

Moving in and maintenance

Owning means every repair is now yours. A common rule of thumb is to set aside 1% of the home’s value per year for maintenance — about $3,600 annually on a $360,000 home. Keep a separate cushion so a surprise expense in year one doesn’t become a crisis.

5. Pre-qualification vs pre-approval

These sound alike but carry very different weight. A pre-qualification is a rough estimate based on numbers you tell the lender — quick, but non-binding. A pre-approval involves the lender actually verifying your income, assets, and credit, and issuing a written commitment for a specific amount. In a competitive market, sellers take pre-approved buyers seriously and often ignore the rest.

Get pre-approved before you shop, and compare offers from at least two or three lenders. Rates and fees vary more than most buyers expect, and multiple mortgage inquiries within a short window count as a single credit pull — so shopping around won’t wreck your score.

6. The buying timeline, step by step

From decision to keys, a typical purchase runs 30–60 days once you’re under contract, but the prep work starts earlier. The usual sequence:

1. Check and build your credit — even a small score bump can lower your rate and your PMI. 2. Save for down payment plus closing costs plus a cushion. 3. Get pre-approved and learn your real budget. 4. Shop with an agent who knows your target areas. 5. Make an offer and negotiate. 6. Inspection and appraisal — your chance to catch problems and confirm value. 7. Underwriting — the lender’s final review. 8. Close — sign, pay your down payment and closing costs, and get the keys.

7. Common first-time buyer mistakes

Shopping before pre-approval. You waste time on homes you can’t finance and lose out to prepared buyers. Maxing out the approval. The lender’s ceiling is not your target. Forgetting the full cost of ownership. Taxes, insurance, maintenance, and HOA dues add up fast. Opening new credit before closing. A new car loan or credit card mid-process can sink your approval — make no big financial moves until the deal is done. Skipping the inspection to win a bidding war. It can save the offer but expose you to five-figure surprises.

Start with your number

Before anything else, find out what you can realistically afford — it shapes every decision that follows.
