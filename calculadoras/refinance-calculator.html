The break-even point is the real decision.

If you plan to sell or move before you reach it, refinancing costs you money even at a lower rate. If you'll stay well past it, the savings are yours to keep.

How the refinance calculator works

Refinancing replaces your current mortgage with a new one, ideally at a lower rate. This tool compares the monthly principal-and-interest payment on both loans, then measures how long it takes your monthly savings to repay the closing costs — your break-even point.

Break-even (months) = Closing costs ÷ Monthly savings If closing costs are $4,000 and you save $212 a month, you break even in about 19 months. After that, the savings are real money in your pocket.

Watch the loan term

Refinancing a loan with 27 years left into a fresh 30-year term lowers your payment, but it also restarts the clock. A lower rate can still mean more total interest if you stretch the term back out. This calculator shows lifetime interest for both loans so you can see the full picture, not just the monthly number.

When refinancing usually makes sense

A common rule of thumb is that refinancing is worth considering when you can lower your rate by roughly 0.5 to 1 percentage point and you'll stay in the home past the break-even point. Cash-out refinances, removing PMI, or switching from an adjustable to a fixed rate are other common reasons.

Frequently asked questions

What is a break-even point?

It's the number of months it takes for your monthly savings to repay the closing costs of refinancing. Before that point you're still recovering fees; after it, the savings are yours.

Does a lower rate always save money?

Not always. If you extend the term or plan to move before breaking even, a lower rate can still cost you more overall. Look at both the break-even point and the lifetime interest figures shown above.

What are typical closing costs to refinance?

Refinance closing costs usually run about 2% to 5% of the loan amount, covering the appraisal, origination, title and other fees. Some lenders offer "no-closing-cost" refinances that fold the fees into a higher rate.

Should I refinance into a shorter term?

A shorter term, like moving from 30 to 15 years, usually raises your monthly payment but sharply cuts total interest and builds equity faster. Try both terms above to compare.
