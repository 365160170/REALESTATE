Common ceiling for approval

How much house can I afford?

Lenders decide how much you can borrow using your debt-to-income ratio (DTI) — the share of your gross monthly income that goes toward debt. This calculator works backward from that limit: it finds the largest monthly housing payment your income can support, then converts it into a maximum home price for your rate, term and down payment.

Max housing payment = (Income ÷ 12 × DTI) − Monthly debts The remaining payment is split into principal, interest, taxes and insurance, then reverse-engineered into a loan amount and home price.

What counts as monthly debt?

Include recurring obligations that appear on your credit report: car loans, student loans, minimum credit-card payments, personal loans and child support. Everyday expenses like groceries, utilities and subscriptions are not counted in DTI, even though they still affect your real budget.

The 28/36 rule, explained

A common guideline is the 28/36 rule: spend no more than 28% of gross monthly income on housing, and no more than 36% on total debt including housing. Many loan programs allow higher back-end ratios — FHA loans sometimes reach 43% or beyond — but staying near 36% leaves real room in your monthly budget.

The tool above shows the lender's ceiling. Your own comfort level may sit lower once you factor in savings, retirement, travel and emergencies. Treat the result as a maximum, not a target.

Frequently asked questions

What is a debt-to-income ratio?

DTI is the percentage of your gross monthly income that goes toward debt payments. Lenders use it to judge whether you can take on a mortgage. A lower DTI generally means you can borrow more and qualify for better terms.

What income should I enter?

Use your gross annual household income before taxes — including salary, reliable bonuses and any co-borrower's income. Lenders qualify you on gross, not take-home, pay.

Why does my down payment change how much I can afford?

Your monthly payment sets the size of the loan you can carry. The down payment is added on top of that loan to reach the total home price — so more cash down means a higher price for the same monthly payment.

Does a higher DTI limit mean I should borrow more?

Not necessarily. A higher limit lets you qualify for a larger loan, but it also leaves less cushion for savings and unexpected costs. Many buyers deliberately borrow below their maximum.

Are property taxes and insurance included?

Yes. The maximum payment shown is a full PITI estimate — principal, interest, property tax and home insurance — so the resulting price reflects your true monthly cost.

Is this financial advice?

No. This tool is for informational purposes only. Your actual approval depends on your credit, employment, assets and lender guidelines. Confirm figures with a licensed professional.
