Renting isn't "throwing money away" — and buying isn't always the win.

The break-even year is what matters.

Buying carries big upfront costs — down payment, closing, and years of maintenance. It usually takes several years for the equity you build and rent you avoid to overtake those costs. Leave before the break-even year and renting often wins.

How the rent vs buy calculator works

This tool adds up the real cost of each path over the years you choose. For buying, that means your down payment, closing costs, mortgage payments and ongoing costs like tax, insurance and upkeep — minus the equity and home appreciation you gain. For renting, it's your rent over time, growing each year, with your down payment assumed to stay invested.

Net cost of buying = Upfront + Payments + Ownership costs − Equity − Appreciation The break-even year is the first year the running cost of buying drops below the running cost of renting.

Why the time horizon changes everything

The single biggest factor is how long you stay. Over one or two years, the closing costs and agent fees of buying and later selling dominate, and renting almost always wins. Over ten or more years, equity and appreciation usually tip it toward buying. Move the "years to compare" slider to watch the verdict flip.

What this calculator can't know

It uses steady annual assumptions, but real markets move in cycles. Home appreciation, rent growth and what you'd earn by investing your down payment instead are all uncertain. Treat the result as a well-informed estimate, not a guarantee.

Frequently asked questions

Is renting really throwing money away?

No. Renting buys you flexibility and freedom from maintenance, and the down payment you didn't spend can be invested. Whether buying beats renting depends mostly on how long you stay and local prices.

What is the break-even year?

It's the first year the total cost of buying falls below the total cost of renting. Before it, renting is cheaper; after it, owning pulls ahead. If you'll move before that year, renting usually makes more financial sense.

Does this include maintenance and taxes?

Yes. The "costs" percentage bundles property tax, insurance and upkeep as a yearly share of the home's value, so the buying side reflects the full cost of ownership, not just the mortgage.

What appreciation rate should I use?

US home prices have historically risen roughly 3–4% per year on average, but this varies widely by market and period. Try a conservative and an optimistic figure to see how sensitive the result is.

Is this financial advice?

No. This tool is for informational purposes only and relies on assumptions that may not match your market. Confirm the numbers with a qualified professional before deciding.
